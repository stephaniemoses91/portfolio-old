Statement of Philosophy

I have looked up to teachers all my life as they shoulder the ultimate responsibility of preparing children to become good citizens who meaningfully contribute to society. Hence, choosing to become a teacher after spending ten years in marketing and public relations has been one of the best and apt decisions I have made as an adult. I can proudly say that my Elementary School Math teacher played an important role in this decision. I was a very withdrawn student with severe anxiety issues as a ten-year-old. I battled with low self-confidence and inferiority complex for years due to certain family circumstances. While most teachers labelled me as ‘incapable and lazy’, my Math teacher saw me differently. She understood that I was going through something and chose to focus and build on my positives. She engaged me in several extra-curricular activities like drama and debate which helped distract me from my personal struggles and built my confidence and morale. She helped me discover my unique strengths and capabilities and I want to have the same impact on my students.


It is a shame that most often teaching is solely associated with preparing students for exams; I strongly disagree with this notion as students need more than academic knowledge and guidance. I believe that a teacher’s role is to focus on students’ academic and affective learning goals. I would explicitly teach academic and affective skills and promote a positive and nurturing learning environment where all students feel safe, valued and a sense of community.


I envision myself in a highly diverse classroom filled with students from different cultural backgrounds, socio-economic statuses and a diverse range of learning abilities and exceptionalities. I understand that I cannot be an effective teacher and reach all my students with a ‘one-size fits all’ instructional approach. Instead, I need to personally get to know each of my students and dedicate adequate time and effort towards exploring their diverse strengths which will help me purposefully differentiate my instruction. According to Howard Gardener’s Theory of Multiple Intelligences, individuals possess eight or more relatively autonomous intelligences and they can be identified as linguistic, logical-mathematical, spatial, musical, bodily-kinesthetic, naturalistic, interpersonal and intrapersonal. The theory strongly resonates with me as it transforms the conventional perceptions of intelligence and leads to the recognition of diverse abilities that account for a broader range of human potential. I would incorporate multi-ability tasks in the learning environment and allow students to demonstrate their knowledge and understanding in multiple ways, promoting an inclusive environment conducive to learning.


I strongly believe that students too have an important role to play in education; they should not be viewed as empty vessels. They are partners in the learning process who come to the classroom with a wealth of prior knowledge and experience which help enrich and enhance the learning experience.


Finally, teachers are the most powerful and immediate role-models for children. They develop acceptable standards of behaviour in their mind by observing how we treat students and adults in the community. Hence, I would mindfully model positive social interactions in the way I address each student in class, the tone of voice I use in the classroom and in the way I make intervention when I witness challenging behaviour in the classroom.
